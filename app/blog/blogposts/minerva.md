### So what is Minerva anyway? 
Minerva is a fully accredited, 4-year undergraduate university based out of San Francisco, with an acceptance rate of ~1%.

Following are key components of Minerva‚Äôs education model: 

- **Global Travel:** Minerva has a mandatory global travel program. Each cohort must travel to a different country *every semester*, and the locations are pre-determined. Freshman are always together in a country at any point in time, and so are the sophomores, juniors and seniors. We call it the global rotation, and it goes like this (for class of 2022):
    - Freshman Year: San Francisco
    - Sophomore Year: Seoul & Hyderabad
    - Junior Year: Berlin & Buenos Aires
    - Senior Year: London & Taipei
- **Diverse Community:** Despite being an American liberal arts college, Minerva has a 75% international student body, hailing from 50+ countries. In addition, in every country we go to, we are welcomed by a local staff team that organizes events, activities, and internships for us in partnership with local organizations and professionals. Therefore, in addition to being part of a highly diverse student body, we also get to engage meaningfully with local communities and organizations in 7 different countries.
- **Academic Curriculum:** This is the crux of why Minerva was created: to revitalize and revolutionize higher education. Our entire curriculum is built according to the latest education research and *science of learning* principles. Every class is designed to maximize student learning. I know this sounds like classic marketing material for any other university, but I am going to demonstrate to you through this article why these are not just bloated claims. In addition to a very intentional curriculum design, each class comes with a location-based assignment, where we are required to interact meaningfully with the context of the location we are in. For example, a geology assignment might require a student to collect rock samples of their rotation city for geological analysis, or a marketing assignment requiring students to interact with the marketing department of a local business to write a case study.
- **Software:** In order to deliver a science-of-learning-optimized education, Minerva has created an active-learning forum, which is a bespoke platform for empowering professors to teach and students to learn in the best way possible. It enables us to have highly interactive seminar-style classes, capped at 20 students. No, we don‚Äôt have lectures and we don‚Äôt have tests. But I promise you this software has helped me absorb knowledge in a way that no test or lecture could possibly achieve. And if you are thinking to yourself, ‚ÄúS*ounds like online classes on Zoom*‚Äù, then I can tell you from personal experience that it is a million times better. In fact, many of us would prefer learning on this platform over in-person classes when paired with a learning-optimized curriculum design.

### ~~Hyderabad~~  Bali and the Pandemic

The pandemic hit. The world halted. I was sipping coconuts in Bali. It was bizarre.

Bali is not a Minerva city, but I went there when the rest of my cohort went to India. As a Pakistani, I was advised against going to India due to political tensions. 

While many of my fellow classmates were stuck in India and Argentina, I was *stuck* in Bali.

I admit I got lucky, but I was also terrified of being alone on an island while the future of the world became deeply uncertain. My classmates had each other, but I just had myself because I went to Bali alone. And so I learned: 

- To find myself a community of nomadic women, who supported me emotionally and physically when the going got tough. For example, when I got the flu and was unable to move, my dear friends left lots of food and flowers at my doorstep so I didn‚Äôt have to worry about it.
- To have a hobby that made me feel alive. I chose calisthenics, design, and creative modeling. I took on my first big freelance project as a web designer for an Italian luxury food company.

Of course the coconuts, beaches, and swimming pools helped, but no amount of beach can give you emotional strength the way faith can.

While all of this was happening, I continued to take classes. Since our classes are hosted on the active learning forum, our academics were not affected in the slightest. 


### Heart-Centered Learning

You are probably wondering at this point about what I decided to major in, since I was no longer going to do design. In sophomore year, I went full explore mode. I took the classes I wanted to, with little thought as to what major it would make. I wanted to take a bottom-up approach to figure out what excites me the most. I took classes in geology, finance, philosophy, causal inference, neuroscience and the likes. 

And it was glorious. I thoroughly enjoyed learning, in a way that I have never done so before. And I suspect it has something to do with the science of learning principles that are baked into a Minerva education. We don‚Äôt have lectures, and we don‚Äôt have traditional tests, but we do have a unique format of classes that leave students with no option but to learn.  

Before we attend a class, we must do pre-class work, which is basically homework. Sounds pretty standard, right? The thing is, doing the pre-class work is critical to attending a class. At the start of class, you must submit your pre-class work and if you don‚Äôt, then you‚Äôre marked absent. During class, you engage in a highly interactive discussion with the teacher and other students that builds upon the pre-class work. Unlike lectures, you can‚Äôt just mentally zone out of Minerva classes, because the platform measures the total talk time of each student, and the professor can easily call on you anytime. You can‚Äôt turn off your camera either. You must be there in all of your presence, and you must engage. It might not be everyone‚Äôs cup of tea, but this kind of highly interactive seminar-style class design is my favorite way of learning. 

There is a lot that I am leaving out for the sake of brevity (trust me I am trying *really hard* to be concise even if it doesn‚Äôt seem like it). At it‚Äôs core, Minerva education is built in a highly intentional way and the educational principles are continuously updated based on student feedback and the latest research in education science. And I love that because it is significantly more student-oriented than any other major university that I know of in the United States.

I do believe that learning is a two-way street, because the best education system in the world cannot leave a dent on a mind that isn‚Äôt open. I personally approached my learning in an almost primal, animalistic way. Alongside an open-mind, I brought an open heart and let myself *feel* everything that I learned. It was exhilarating.

Funnily enough, one of the science of learning principles  is called ‚Äúaffective context‚Äù. It is the idea that emotional associations tend to improve learning and memory retention. 

The rate at which I was absorbing knowledge was unprecedented, and I was having an incredible time learning in this way. I don‚Äôt think every Minerva student felt this way about our classes, and I can‚Äôt say for sure that the curriculum design intended for this, but this is one of the ways that my undergrad felt thoroughly invigorating to not just to my mind, but also my feeling self. 

Having explored all that interested me in the span of time that was available to me, I realized that I found the concept of consciousness and the process of creating it out of thin air (chips, in this case), particularly interesting. ### ~~Buenos Aires & Taipei~~   London & Berlin: Reflections

Unfortunately due to the pandemic, we could not go to Buenos Aires and Taipei, but we did spend a semester each in London and Berlin.

Being a senior, I reflected back on my four years and realized that despite a strong pandemic-induced disruption, I grew so much as a result of the global travel, academics, and professional opportunities that came through Minerva. At the same time, I started to feel the downsides of being on the road perpetually while figuring out my life and holding on to friendships and connections I made with people along the way. 

Here are some of my reflections: 

- **Find the kind of travel that feels energy-giving to *you*:**
I like slow travel significantly more than vacations, tourism, and any trip that last less than a month. Ideally, I‚Äôd prefer to not buy a round-trip ticket at all. Living in places for months on end, while finding ways to integrate into the local culture is deeply transformative for me. In fact, I wish Minerva‚Äôs 4 years/7 cities model was instead a 4 years/4 cities model, because a semester felt so short to me. But at the same time, I have classmates who loved to travel around even when they were already traveling as part of the global rotation. For example, lots of Minervans went to Japan and Thailand for weekend trips during our time in Korea.
- **Diversity of experiences is essential to growth:**
The sheer diversity of my experiences give me a personal and professional robustness that I would not trade for a spot at any of the ivy leagues. At this point, I‚Äôve worked with people/companies from Japan, Italy, Argentina, United States, and India. I have mentored 20+ students. I‚Äôve collaborated within a really complex network of teams to launch a community platform for 1500+ travelers. I have become someone who can walk up to anyone, in any room, anywhere in the world, and strike up a memorable conversation. I have the confidence to navigate any kind of difficult situation given any amount of resources. I am able to communicate effectively no matter how complicated the concept, and I have the ability to see unintuitive connections between very complex ideas floating in the world.
- **Find a healthy balance of novelty and predictability to feel good in life:** 
Living an intense life of novelty for 4 years felt incredibly tiring. It was hard to date or make new friends because why would anyone invest in a relationship with a deadline? The easiest friendships were with classmates who were in the same boat (plane, actually), as well as travelers who found transience more comfortable. In every city, I found a cafe and quickly became a regular there. I built friendships with cafe staff, and they‚Äôd give me little gifts every now and then. I craved consistency, belonging, and comfort because I was always living outside my comfort zone, and my world was always changing. Throughout senior year, I craved to sign a year-long lease so I can stay put in one location for more than 4 months. I was looking forward to the mundane tasks of buying furniture and plants, and not having to move again for at least a year.
- **Letting go of notions of superiority and inferiority is important to being a healthy human being:**
Minerva tends to attract some of the smartest, most insightful and uniquely-exceptional students from around the world. Entering Minerva after a successful academic career as a type-A overachiever with a trail of awards and scholarships behind me, I was doomed to ride the wave of imposter syndrome. Suddenly, I was no longer the coolest person in the room, because the girl to the left of me had published a book, and the guy to the right of me had sold a company, and the person behind me had solved an important math problem for the world. But overtime, I realized that I cannot put myself and all of my classmates on a linear scale of success. All of us have been dealt a different set of cards in life, and all of us bring a different kind of beauty and uniqueness to the way that we participate in and respond to the world around us.

No amount of storytelling feels sufficient. The limits of language, time, existence, emotion, cognition, and eyeballs will haunt me, but I promised myself I will create and let my creations live in the world rent free. I hope to share more stories in the future. Thank you for being here üíô
